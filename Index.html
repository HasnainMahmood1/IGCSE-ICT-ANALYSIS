📉Long-term downward trend

The A* percentage has broadly fallen over 10 years. June sessions dropped from ~79% in 2015 to ~67% in 2025 — a 12-percentage-point decline. November sessions dropped from ~79% in 2015 to ~68–71% in 2025.

📐Syllabus restructure (2023)

The 2023 syllabus change (max 300 → 280) coincided with a visible reset in raw thresholds. Despite different numbers, the percentage required remained broadly similar, confirming Cambridge's standardisation process works across formats.

🔁June vs November pattern

June sessions are generally slightly harder than November (higher A* threshold %). This is consistent across most years, likely reflecting a stronger cohort of June candidates vs the more selective November entry.

⚠️2020 anomaly

November 2020 showed a highly unusual split — option EX required 237/300 (79%) while EY/EZ required only 216/300 (72%). This extreme variance likely reflects marking adjustments during the COVID-19 examination disruption period.

📊June 2015 — Hardest ever

With thresholds reaching 238/300 (79.3%), June 2015 was the most demanding June session in the 10-year window. Paper 1 theory boundaries were also notably high that year.

📊June 2025 — Easiest ever

June 2025 saw the lowest A* threshold at 188/280 (67.1%) — a new record low. The A grade was also at its lowest, suggesting an overall easier paper or marking adjustment.

⚡Nov 2020 — COVID outlier

Option EX required 237/300 (79%) while EY/EZ required only 216/300 (72%). This 21-mark gap between options within the same session is extraordinary and almost certainly related to assessment adaptations during COVID-19.

📉Post-2023 stabilisation

After the syllabus restructure, November thresholds have stabilised at 191–222/280 (68–79%). The new paper structure appears to generate more consistent boundaries than the old 300-mark format did.

📈The steady decline

A* thresholds have broadly fallen over the decade. June sessions dropped ~12 percentage points from 2015 to 2025. November dropped roughly 8–9 points. This could reflect easier papers, more generous marking, or a widening candidate pool.

📅Year-by-year volatility

The most volatile years were 2020 (COVID) and 2015 (unusually high). Within a typical year, the spread between the hardest and easiest option variants is 10–20 raw marks. Post-2023, spreads within a session narrowed considerably.

🔄Syllabus era comparison

Old syllabus (2015–2022, max 300): average A* ~72–76% of total. New syllabus (2023–2025, max 280): average A* ~68–72% of total. The new syllabus consistently produces lower % thresholds, suggesting either simpler papers or recalibration by Cambridge.

💡Strategic implication

For students: targeting ~75% of total marks is a safe A* buffer in any year. The absolute floor has been 67% (June 2025) and ceiling 79% (Jun 2015 / Nov 2020 EX). Planning for 75% gives solid margin across all observed outcomes.
